NASA's New Inflatable Heat Shield Revolutionizes Mars Landings

Designed by NASA's Langley Research Centre, this innovative heat shield can inflate while in Earth orbit and safely return to the planet. In late 2022, the concept of an inflatable heat shield took flight for the first time, successfully demonstrating its potential for future Earth re-entry missions. One of the biggest challenges in launching rockets isn't the weight of the payload but the space it takes up. This limits what rockets can carry, increasing costs and complexity in space missions. To solve this, NASA developed a heat shield that inflates in space, saving valuable room for other equipment.

After re-entering Earth's atmosphere, LOFTID doesn't rely on parachutes to slow down before splashdown. Wouldn’t that risk be destroying the payload or harming a crew? Not necessarily. Thanks to its large surface area and lightweight design, the shield acts like an air brake, slowing to a splashdown speed of 5.8 meters per second. For comparison, a Soyuz MS capsule lands at about 2.0 meters per second. As mentioned, the inflatable heat shield saves space on the 7-month journey from Earth to Mars. It can also be scaled down to reduce weight, which is especially useful given Mars’ extremely thin atmosphere. For context, Earth’s atmospheric pressure is 101.325 kPa, while Mars has just 0.61 kPa. That means entering Mars’ atmosphere involves less heat and pressure, making the descent somewhat easier and less risky than on Earth.
